# Vertex Radar — Positioning Brief

Master value-prop reference. Pull from this for landing page copy, Etsy listing description, ad copy, and future tracker launches — don't reinvent the pitch each time.

## What Vertex Radar is

A suite of dynamic business trackers for Amazon and e-commerce sellers, built by a 24-year operator rather than a generic software vendor. It ships in two tiers — a static one-time Excel workbook (Etsy, no login) and a connected version that lives in the seller's own Google Sheet with automatic alerts — both running on the same underlying engine: dynamic fields the seller customizes, automatic calculation, export anywhere. The first product, the Inventory Aging & Dead Stock Tracker, is fully built: it pulls Last Sale Date and Date Received automatically from Amazon's own sales and shipment reports (no manual entry), ages every SKU into an Active/Watch/Discount/Liquidate/Dead status, and — beyond just showing what's stuck — calculates what it's actually costing to leave it there. PPC Performance, FBA Margin & Fee, and Launch Unit Economics trackers are next, sharing the same engine.

## The challenge Amazon sellers face

- Cash quietly disappears into dead stock, and most sellers don't know the scale of it until it's a crisis — a single account audit surfaced $7.9M+ in dead inventory that had been invisible in normal reporting.
- Manual tracking doesn't survive contact with a busy operator. A spreadsheet that requires updating by hand gets abandoned within a few weeks, which quietly breaks the entire premise of "aging" analysis — it's only accurate if someone keeps feeding it dates.
- Existing tools have real gaps. Helium 10 and Jungle Scout are strong on product research and PPC, but neither has a dedicated FBA packaging/size-tier fee optimizer or a true dead-stock carrying-cost model — sellers either eyeball this manually or don't do it at all.
- The real cost of dead stock is invisible. A number like "$31,900 stuck" doesn't create urgency. What it's costing every month it sits there — capital, storage, risk — does.
- Data is fragmented across disconnected reports and tools, with no single operational view that turns raw numbers into a next action.

## What Vertex Radar offers to bridge that gap

- Automation over upkeep — dates pull straight from Amazon's own reports, so the tracker stays accurate without becoming another abandoned spreadsheet.
- A recommended action, not just a number — every SKU gets a status and a plain-language next step, turning a report into a decision.
- The true cost of inaction, quantified — carrying cost turns "this is stuck" into "this is costing you $X/month," which is what actually drives someone to act.
- Operator-built credibility — 24 years running Amazon, Walmart, eBay, and DTC operations, not a dev team guessing at seller pain points.
- Flexible entry point — buy once and own it, or go connected for live alerts; the product meets sellers at whatever level of commitment they're ready for.
- A suite that grows, not a point tool — the same engine extends to PPC, margin, and launch economics, so it becomes one system instead of five disconnected subscriptions.

## Channel-specific rules

- **Etsy shop About section:** single-product only. Etsy buyers are making a quick, low-stakes decision on the one thing they can actually buy there — don't mention the suite, "first product," or unreleased trackers. Draw only from the Inventory Aging Tracker specifics above (auto-pulled dates, Active/Watch/Discount/Liquidate/Dead, carrying-cost calc) and the challenge/offer points that apply to that one product.
- **vertexradar.com:** full suite framing is appropriate — that's the house brand, and visitors there are evaluating the company, not just one SKU.
